Bryant Ball Game

Bryant Le, also known as Baren Bui, must catch the falling Wilson heads and
dodge the falling T-poses in the spooky forest in order to preserve his
Alpha Leo position. Catching a Wilson head adds 5 points to the score,
while failing to capture one reduces the life count by 1 point. Touching
a T-pose reduces the life count by 2, while dodging them increases the
life count by .1 points. A random variable is used when generating Wilson
heads and T-poses. The game gets harder as the player progresses and gets
a higher scores by making the Wilson heads and T-poses fall faster. The
game ends when the player allows too many Wilson heads to pass or touches
too many T-poses.

We decided to create a game based on our fellow classmate, deriving inspiration
from Bryant's funny Alpha Leo video resume. We were successful in creating
a random sprite generator from a certain x-range, and in creating a smooth
transition between backgrounds. However, we met difficulties in making the
sprites fall and in balancing the spawn ratios. We overcame these obstacles
through the use of if-then statements. If we had more time, we would change
Bryant's speed to match the difficulty change.

This line of code is an algorithm because it allows the computer to run
a line of commands when the sprite is duplicated. This algorithm allows
the Wilson heads and T-poses to spawn from a random x-value after the sprite
is cloned. These clones then fall at a constant rate while spinning, and
changes the score by 5 when it touches Bryant. When it touches the edge,
it reduces the Life count by 1.

Ryan Simulator

Ryan Simulator is a retelling of a real life experience of Ryan Trang,
currently a sophomore at La Quinta High School. The story begins in
McGarvin Intermediate and progresses into freshmen year as Ryan
finds multiple love interests. However, Ryan is unable to become
anything greater than platonic, and he must turn to tennis to compensate
for his sadness.

While making Ryan Simulator, I encountered difficulty with connecting the
different scenes. When I connected the multiple scenes, I tried setting
up all of the events at once, but in doing so, I could not run the
program to check my progress. So, I was forced to do every event
individually. One of the iterative processes that I incorporated into
the program was when the user chose the "no" option using the input
function. Everytime they chose "no", they would return to an entirely
different story path where Ryan would choose to hang out with his friends
and play tennis.

Organizing the scenes of my story is a form of procedural abstraction
since the storyline is simplified into a series of functions. These functions
can be reused anywhere inside of the code and always have the same outcome.
For example, everytime the user entered a "no" option in Ryan Simulator,
the "bromies()" function would play regardless of where it was. Abstracting
the scenes makes it simpler for the programmer to follow, since the scenes
are organized in a sequential fashion. Also, in the if-statements based
on user-input, using the events as functions makes the code cleaner in
comparison to having an entire storyline within one if-statement.
